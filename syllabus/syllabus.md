Class Structure
---------------
This course is composed into mini-sections to give you more opportunity to interact with both me and your peers during our class sessions.  I believe this communal process of working through the topic of the course together is central to the approach MSOE takes to learning and I'm grateful for the opportunity to structure the course this way this term.

During your once-a-week meeting, we will have an hour of project time followed by an hour of lecture.  Depending on the project of the week, I may have project-specific activities that we work together or it may be a synchronized time where you all work on projects and I am available for questions or extra-class discussions.

Reading reflections will be due before the start of Monday's class.  Class time will be spent discussing the reading, giving you an opportunity to ask questions about reading reflection questions you were unsure of or to further discuss the reading topics which are the core topics of the course.

To get credit for submitting your reading reflections, you must attend the discussion that follows the reading.  During this discussion, you must have your camera on and be ready to unmute and join the discussion.  Each student can miss one class discussion and still get credit for the reading, although they still must submit the reading on time for full credit.  After this exception, to get credit for a reading and missed class, you must submit both the reading and a 10-minute video of you discussing the reading and your thoughts on the reading questions.

Topics
------

This is predominantly a reading-reflection-driven course.  And YOU will have an influence on what questions are asked on reading reflections... and exams.

I will provide about two-thirds of the outcomes for the course, and you, as a class, will be responsible for determining the remaining outcomes.

My main goal in this course is to guide you into *understanding* how the most capable neural networks available today work.  I also want to guide you into *caring* about how neural networks impact people and the planet.  But neither of these goals can be measured through exams or quizzes.  I will *not* assess either of these directly.

Instead, I will use *reading reflection questions* -- questions you can answer on readings, half-exams, and the final exam, to demonstrate your knowledge of the material we are discussing in this course.  

You will be required to write some reading reflection questions on topics that are of interest to you related to LLMs or the readings.  You will be asked to write some questions you *can* answer, as well as some questions you *cannot* yet answer.  Your questions may make their way into the question pools for the exams, where you and your peers will have opportunity to comment on them and discuss them before they end up on exams. 

Some of these questions may be adopted by the class and eventually end up on one of the half-exams or final exam. 

Some reading reflection questions will be placed directly onto an exam or a quiz from the question pool.  While not all exam questions will be drawn from the reflection questions exactly, my intent is to have there be no surprises by drawing and writing fairly closely from the questions we have discussed as a class and that you are have answered during your readings.  For this reason, notesheets will NOT be allowed on exams.

Lab Projects
------------
In addition to the readings, you will have weekly lab projects.

These projects are:

*  *LLM internals and interpretability* (Implementing Google’s gradscope on Google Gemma or another model we can fit on a T4.)
*  *Training an LLM and Chain of Thought*: Training an LLM on a small-domain task (nested arithmetic) for the purposes of:
   *	Seeing the process of training an LLM from scratch
   *	Analyzing attention and possibly other interpretability patterns on the LLM.
*  *Evaluating LLMs*: We will study how LLMs can be evaluated reliably despite their stochstic, fallible nature.
